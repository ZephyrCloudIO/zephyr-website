Every engineering organization invests in two things: a CI/CD pipeline that packages and tests software, and a CDN that serves it to users. These are well-understood problems with mature tooling. Hundreds of companies compete for your budget in both categories.

But there is a layer between them that almost no organization has deliberately built — and its absence quietly taxes every release you ship.

That layer is **deployment orchestration**: the logic that decides what version of what application goes where, under what conditions, with what audit trail, and across which infrastructure providers. In most stacks, this layer is improvised. It lives in pipeline YAML written by someone who has since left the company, in Slack messages between a DevOps engineer and a product manager, and in undocumented tribal knowledge that surfaces only when something breaks at 2am.

This post is about why that gap matters more as your organization scales — and what it looks like when you close it.

## The Modern Deployment Problem Has Changed

A few years ago, deploying a web application meant deploying one thing. A monolith, a server-rendered page, a single-page app. The deployment story was simple: build it, upload it, point your DNS at it.

That is no longer the world most engineering organizations live in.

The rise of **micro-frontend architectures** means organizations now manage dozens of independently deployable frontend units — each owned by a different team, on a different release cadence, with different dependencies. A "deploy" is no longer one action. It is a coordination problem.

At the same time, **CDN vendor lock-in** has become a real and underappreciated risk. The organizations that negotiated a single CDN contract five years ago now find themselves with reduced leverage at renewal time, limited by regional coverage gaps, and exposed to outage risk concentrated in one provider. Moving is hard because their deployment process is tightly coupled to that vendor's primitives.

And increasingly, **compliance and audit requirements** are expanding beyond backend systems. Regulated industries — financial services, healthcare, government — are starting to ask frontend infrastructure the same questions they ask databases: what version was deployed when? Who authorized it? What changed? Most organizations cannot answer these questions without a manual archaeology exercise through git history and pipeline logs.

The result is that deployment has become a coordination problem masquerading as a tooling problem. Teams reach for better CI/CD pipelines, better CDN features, better deployment scripts — when what they actually need is a different architectural layer.

## What Deployment Orchestration Actually Means

Orchestration is an overloaded word. In the context of deployment, it has a specific meaning with four distinct components.

**Version registration** is the foundation. Every build should produce an immutable, content-addressed artifact with a unique identity. Not a mutable artifact that gets overwritten on each deploy. An immutable snapshot that can be referenced, compared, rolled back to, and audited — forever. This is how backend deployments have worked for years with container images and artifact registries. Frontend and edge deployments are catching up.

**Environment and tag routing** is the rules engine. Which version of an application lands in production? Which lands in staging? Which is served to a specific user segment for a canary rollout? These decisions should be governed by explicit, auditable rules — not hardcoded pipeline logic or manual processes. The rules should be owned by your platform team, not embedded in every individual application's CI configuration.

**Multi-CDN publishing** is the infrastructure abstraction. The same version of an application should be deployable to any CDN provider — or multiple simultaneously — without any code change. This is what breaks vendor lock-in at the architectural level, not at the contract level.

**Audit logging** closes the loop. Every state transition — a new version registered, a tag updated, an environment pointed to a new version — should be recorded permanently and be queryable by the teams who need it: platform engineers, security, compliance, and leadership.

These are not new concepts. They exist in every mature backend deployment platform. Kubernetes has them. AWS ECS has them. The enterprise on-call engineer has relied on them for years. What is new is bringing them to frontend and edge deployments.

## How Zephyr Implements the Orchestration Layer

Zephyr is built as three distinct stages, each with a clear responsibility boundary.

**Stage 0: Build-time.** A lightweight plugin integrates with your existing bundler — Rspack, Webpack, Vite, Rollup, Rsbuild, Astro, or any of the 13 supported toolchains. It wraps your build, computes a content-addressed identity for every asset, uploads only what has changed (deduplication means unchanged files are never re-uploaded), and hands a build manifest to the control plane. No rewrite of your build system. No migration.

**Stage 1: Orchestration.** The control plane receives the build manifest and executes the orchestration logic. It registers the new version as an immutable snapshot, evaluates tag and environment rules to determine publish targets, computes the multi-CDN distribution plan, and emits deployment events. This is where governance lives. The entity hierarchy — organization → project → application → version → environment — maps directly to how engineering organizations are actually structured, giving platform teams the controls they need without constraining individual application teams.

**Stage 2: Edge serving.** Assets are served from Cloudflare Workers — globally distributed, no cold starts, sub-50ms delivery worldwide. Storage is content-addressed: the same file stored in one version is not duplicated in the next version if it has not changed. The same asset stored across ten applications is stored once.

The result for a deployment is that seven CDN providers — Cloudflare, AWS, Akamai, Fastly, Netlify, Kubernetes, and custom webhook targets — are available as configuration choices, not infrastructure decisions. You can deploy the same application to multiple providers simultaneously. You can shift traffic between providers by changing configuration, not by redeploying your application.

## What This Means for Your Organization

The organizational impact of closing the orchestration gap shows up in four areas.

**Release velocity.** Content-addressed deduplication means the time to deploy is proportional to what changed, not to the total size of your application. A team that ships ten small changes ships ten fast deploys. A team that has been bundling changes to avoid slow deploys can stop doing that.

**Infrastructure risk.** Vendor diversification becomes a configuration decision rather than a re-architecture project. Your CDN negotiating position improves when switching providers is not a six-month engineering project. Regional coverage gaps can be filled by adding a secondary provider without replacing your primary.

**Deployment auditability.** The audit log is built into the platform. When a security team asks "what was deployed in our production environment on March 14th at 9am?" — the answer is a query, not an archaeology project. When a compliance auditor asks for evidence of change control processes — the version history is the evidence.

**Developer autonomy within platform governance.** The most common tension in platform engineering is between central governance and team autonomy. Teams want to move fast. Platform teams need to maintain standards and visibility. Zephyr resolves this by separating concerns: teams own their builds and their application code, the platform owns the deployment rules and the infrastructure abstraction. Neither side compromises.

## The Layer That Was Missing

The organizations that will ship fastest in the coming years are not the ones with the best CI/CD pipelines or the most aggressive CDN contracts. They are the ones that treated deployment orchestration as a first-class infrastructure investment — and built the governance layer that lets teams move fast without moving blind.

Your build pipeline and your CDN are not the same thing. Between them is a gap. Most organizations have been filling that gap with scripts and hope. There is a better architecture.
